# Sign Types

## The Logic of Scale: Map Types

Effective wayfinding at the University of Colorado Boulder is not merely a matter of placing markers in the landscape; it is a coordinated exercise in spatial psychology designed to mirror the natural process of human orientation. We recognize that the human brain possesses a limited capacity for processing new environments, and that an "all-inclusive" map—one that attempts to answer every possible question simultaneously—is an artifact that invites cognitive paralysis. When a user is forced to filter out ninety percent of available data to find the relevant ten percent, the resulting "cognitive load" leads to hesitation and environmental anxiety. To counter this, our mapping strategy is built upon the principle of progressive disclosure, a methodology that ensures the right layer of information is revealed at exactly the right moment in the user’s journey. By treating our maps as a nested sequence of steps rather than a static inventory, we provide the navigational cues necessary for visitors to build a reliable "mental model" of the campus, fostering a sense of institutional hospitality and personal confidence.

The first tier of this system is the Overall Campus Map, a small-scale visualization designed for broad orientation rather than granular precision. At this entry-level stage of navigation, the map functions as a conceptual framework, prioritizing major districts, primary academic hubs, and significant open spaces like The Quad. Because excess detail at this scale would undermine orientation and slow comprehension, we strictly limit building labels to those landmarks that meaningfully anchor the campus structure. Everything else is removed to eliminate visual noise, as the user is scanning for relationships rather than reading for specific room numbers. By focusing on high-level connectors, transit stops, and "You Are Here" context, the Overall Campus Map serves to establish the fundamental mental model required to navigate our complex landscape. This tier of the system is successful when it provides the viewer with enough spatial clarity to answer the foundational question: Where is it?.

As the user transitions from the campus perimeter into a specific precinct, the system shifts into the second tier: the Local Area Map. This medium-scale visualization is designed for active navigation and route confirmation within a quad, district, or cluster of buildings. At this stage, the user has already committed to a destination and no longer requires an overview of the entire university. Instead, they require confirmation that their chosen path is correct. It is at this precise moment that we allow naming density to increase, introducing an expanded set of building names, key secondary paths, and accessible routes relevant to the immediate neighborhood. Detail that would have been distracting at the campus-wide scale now becomes essential for decision-making. By revealing nearby amenities such as student services or restrooms and highlighting clear orientation cues like sightlines and landmarks, the Local Area Map ensures the user remains grounded as they move through internal decision points. This map provides the reassurance necessary to answer the traveler's urgent internal query: Am I going the right way?.

The final tier in our sequence of progressive disclosure is the Building and Adjacency Map, a high-resolution, large-scale visualization of a single building and its immediate surroundings. This scale is dedicated entirely to the task of arrival and the precision required to complete the last fifty to one hundred feet of a journey. Because the user is no longer navigating conceptually but is instead completing a specific task, fine detail now improves usability rather than obscuring it. At this scale, redundant campus context is stripped away to focus the user’s narrow attention on identifying full building names, primary access points, and specific service functions. Critical emphasis is placed on universal design, with a focus on ADA routes and accessible entrances to ensure every visitor can cross the threshold with dignity. By providing a clear orientation marker and precise labeling of immediate adjacencies, this map allows the user to achieve total task completion. It is the final breadcrumb in our trail, designed to answer the user’s ultimate question: How do I finish?
